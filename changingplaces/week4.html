Week 4 — Growing Sydney — Fieldwork

Syllabus focus: managing urban change; fieldwork. Outcomes: GE5-2, GE5-3, GE5-5, GE5-7. Threads: Caretaking Earth, Imagining Innovations.

Week at a glance: L10 works through the Greater Sydney “Metropolis of Three Cities” plan; L11 is the fieldwork lesson — urban heat island temperature measurements plus the biodiversity survey; L12 zooms back out to Australia’s settlement patterns with thinking routines. The fieldwork needs: 3+ thermometers (infrared if available), clipboards, and about 20 minutes outside.
LESSON 10

A Metropolis of Three Cities

Learning intention: we are learning how planners are managing Sydney’s growth through the Metropolis of Three Cities plan.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): “How long does it take to cross Sydney in peak hour?” — introduce the idea of the 30-minute city.
  2. View (15 min): “A Metropolis of Three Cities — The Greater Sydney Region” video/website.
  3. Teach (10 min): the three cities (Eastern Harbour City, Central River City, Western Parkland City) and the plan’s goal that residents live within 30 minutes of jobs and services.
  4. Main task (20 min): comprehension questions (below).
  5. Exit ticket (5 min): name the three cities.

Main task instructions

  1. Comprehension questions: (1) name the three cities of Greater Sydney; (2) define “metropolis”; (3) identify the plan’s four key elements; (4) compare population and tree canopy across the three cities; (5) describe the plan’s overall vision in 2–3 sentences.

External links

  • Search: “A Metropolis of Three Cities Greater Sydney” — plan documents and summary video (NSW Government)

LESSON 11

Urban Sprawl and Heat Islands — FIELDWORK

Learning intention: we are learning how urban surfaces change local temperature and biodiversity, by measuring it ourselves.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): “Why is the school car park hotter than the oval?” — predict, then test.
  2. Fieldwork (25 min): urban heat island investigation — measure and record ground temperature on (a) a hard surface (asphalt/concrete), (b) grass, (c) a shaded area. Three readings per site, record in the fieldwork table, average them.
  3. Analysis (15 min): explain the term “urban heat island” and its causes (dark hard surfaces absorb and re-radiate heat; less vegetation = less evaporative cooling); students propose and rank planning solutions (street trees, light-coloured surfaces, green roofs, more parkland).
  4. Homework/extension: biodiversity survey — record bird species in your yard/local area, investigate why those species are present, and design a backyard or balcony garden to encourage biodiversity.
  5. Wrap (5 min): PMI (plus–minus–interesting) routine on urban sprawl, linked to the Growing Sydney plan; optional cartoon task.

Main task instructions

  1. Complete the fieldwork recording table: three sites × three readings + averages.
  2. Write up: describe the pattern in your data; explain it using the term “urban heat island”; propose three planning solutions and rank them with justification.
  3. This fieldwork satisfies the unit’s fieldwork requirement — keep the completed booklets as evidence.

LESSON 12

Urban Settlement Patterns

Learning intention: we are learning to describe and explain the pattern of where Australians live.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (10 min): See-Think-Wonder routine on a population distribution map of Australia (dot density or night lights image).
  2. Teach (10 min): the factors behind the pattern: climate zones, water availability, transport networks, infrastructure access, topography, European settlement history (coastal ports became capitals).
  3. Main task (20 min): concept map + USA comparison (below).
  4. Exit ticket (5 min): “Australia’s population is concentrated…” — finish the sentence with two reasons.

Main task instructions

  1. Concept map: “Why do Australians live where they do?” — at least five branches (climate, water, transport, infrastructure, topography, history), each with an example.
  2. Comparison task: display a USA population distribution map next to Australia’s. Students write 3–4 sentences: how are the patterns similar/different, and why? (The USA has a populated interior — rivers, rainfall, arable land — Australia largely does not.)
