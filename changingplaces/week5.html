Week 5 — Internal Migration

Syllabus focus: internal migration. Outcomes: GE5-2, GE5-3, GE5-7, GE5-8. Threads: Embracing Diversity.

Week at a glance: L13 builds the vocabulary and the choropleth map skill; L14 covers interstate trends, FIFO work and sea/tree changes; L15 is the COVID-19 internal migration case study — the strongest writing task of the unit so far, in three parts.
LESSON 13

Who Moves Within Australia?

Learning intention: we are learning what internal migration is and how to read a choropleth map of population change.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): class mind map — why might people move WITHIN Australia? Invite students who have moved interstate/regionally to share.
  2. Teach (10 min): key vocabulary for students to copy: migration, temporary, permanent, internal migration, economic.
  3. Skills teach (15 min): choropleth maps — the map is divided into areas of about 10,000 people; the colour scale is NOT centred (e.g. +4.2% growth vs –1.2% decline), so model reading it carefully. Pattern to draw out: net loss from rural areas, net gain in parts of Sydney and Melbourne — urbanisation is the driver.
  4. Main task (15 min): choropleth interpretation questions.
  5. Exit ticket (5 min): define internal migration; one thing a choropleth map shows well and one thing it hides.

Main task instructions

  1. Interpretation questions on the internal migration choropleth: identify two areas of strong growth and two of decline; describe the overall pattern using compass directions and place names; explain the pattern using the word “urbanisation”.

LESSON 14

Trends, FIFO, Sea and Tree Changes

Learning intention: we are learning to describe trends in interstate migration and explain temporary migration, including FIFO work.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): “Which state would you move to, and why?” — quick poll, keep the tally for comparison with the real data.
  2. Data task (15 min): interstate migration chart 2005–2015: identify net-gaining and net-losing states, describe the trends in sentences.
  3. Explore (10 min): SBS interactive population movement website — students browse and record two findings.
  4. Teach (10 min): temporary migration — seasonal work (fruit picking, snow season) and Fly-In Fly-Out mining jobs, including the emotional impact on families (there is a children’s book written for FIFO kids — a humanising detail worth mentioning).
  5. Wrap (10 min): sea change and tree change concepts through guided questions — sets up L15.

Main task instructions

  1. Chart analysis: which states gained, which lost, and how did the pattern change across 2005–2015? Write 3–4 sentences using data (numbers, years).
  2. FIFO discussion questions: why do people take FIFO jobs (push/pull)? What are the costs to families? Is FIFO migration — why/why not?

LESSON 15

COVID-19 and Internal Migration — Case Study

Learning intention: we are learning to analyse a real migration event using push/pull factors, patterns and consequences.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): “What happened to where people wanted to live when everyone could suddenly work from home?”
  2. Read (10 min): the COVID Internal Migration Case Study background reading — the 2020–2022 sea-/tree-change shift out of Sydney and Melbourne into regional Australia: economic drivers (remote work, cheaper housing), social (space, pace of life), environmental (perceived healthier surrounds), political (state border closures); and the partial reversal by 2023 as workers returned to offices and regional prices rose.
  3. Main task (30 min): Parts A–C (below).
  4. Exit ticket (5 min): one push factor and one pull factor from the case study.

Main task instructions

  1. Part A: define sea change and tree change; identify the push and pull factors in the case study; judge which driver was most significant and justify.
  2. Part B: describe the overall migration pattern using “place of origin” and “place of destination”; give one consequence for each (origin cities and destination regions).
  3. Part C: predict and explain the 2023 reverse movement — why did some movers return?
