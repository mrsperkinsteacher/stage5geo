Week 8 — Liveability and Internal Migration in China

Syllabus focus: liveability; internal migration (global case study). Outcomes: GE5-3, GE5-4, GE5-5, GE5-8. Threads: Embracing Diversity, Imitating Humility.

Week at a glance: L22 defines and measures liveability with a rural/urban comparison; L23 is the China internal migration case study — the hukou system and the floating population; L24 is the independent third-country migration report.
LESSON 22

What Makes a Place Liveable?

Learning intention: we are learning to define liveability and compare the liveability of two real places.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): “Name the best place you’ve ever been. What made it good to be in?” — harvest factors on the board.
  2. Teach (10 min): define liveability; mind map measurable factors (housing, transport, healthcare, education, safety, environment, culture).
  3. View (10 min): “Where is the world’s most liveable city?” clip.
  4. Main task (25 min): comparison table + judgement (below).
  5. Exit ticket (5 min): the three factors students personally weight most, ranked.

Main task instructions

  1. Students choose one specific rural town and one specific urban town/city (real places, named).
  2. Complete the structured liveability table comparing them across the class factor list.
  3. Write a 2–3 sentence comparative judgement: which is more liveable, for whom, and why? (Push towards “for whom” — liveability differs by age, income, stage of life.)

External links

  • Search YouTube: “world’s most liveable city” (The Economist/EIU coverage; preview first)

LESSON 23

Internal Migration in China

Learning intention: we are learning how China’s hukou system shapes the lives of internal migrants.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): “Imagine you needed a licence to live in Sydney — and without it, no Medicare, no school for your kids.” That is roughly the hukou system.
  2. Read (15 min): case study read as a class: the hukou household registration system; the “floating population” of internal migrants; the risks they face (no access to healthcare or unemployment benefits, below-minimum-wage pay, risk of detention and deportation to their home village); the 2014 hukou reforms.
  3. Main task (25 min): structured questions (below).
  4. Exit ticket (5 min): define hukou and floating population.

Main task instructions

  1. Structured questions: (1) what is the hukou system and why was it created? (2) who are the floating population and why do they move? (push/pull — apply the unit’s language); (3) what risks do unregistered migrants face? (4) what did the 2014 reforms change, and what did they leave unchanged?
  2. Judgement paragraph: “Do the benefits of China’s internal migration outweigh the costs to migrants?” — describe, explain, judge.

LESSON 24

Third-Country Migration Report

Learning intention: we are learning to research and report on internal migration in a country of our choice.

Lesson outline

  1. Set-up (10 min): students choose a third country (not Australia or China); brainstorm candidates (India, Indonesia, Brazil, Nigeria, USA…). Show the report scaffold and a WAGOLL opening.
  2. Research and write (40 min): one-page report using the set subtitles: Temporary migration · Permanent migration · Reasons · Consequences.
  3. Wrap (5 min): students swap reports and give one star + one wish.

Main task instructions

  1. One-page report on internal migration in the chosen country, under the four set subtitles, with at least one statistic per section and sources listed at the end.
  2. Support: scaffold with sentence starters per subtitle. Extension: evaluate whether that country’s government is helping or hindering its internal migrants.
