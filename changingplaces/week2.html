Week 2 — Megacities

Syllabus focus: consequences of urbanisation — megacities. Outcomes: GE5-2, GE5-3, GE5-7, GE5-8. Threads: Caretaking Earth, Imagining Innovations.

Week at a glance: L4 defines the megacity and researches the current top 10; L5 works through the eight key issues facing megacities with an India focus; L6 is the Asian megacity case study plus the ecological footprint calculator, closing with a consolidation poster.
LESSON 4

Rise of the Megacity

Learning intention: we are learning what a megacity is and where the world’s megacities are.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): guess the population of Tokyo, then reveal. “At what point does a city become a MEGAcity?”
  2. Teach (15 min): definition — a megacity has more than 10 million people. In the 1950s only a handful existed; today there are 30–40+, most in Asia. Unpack examples (Seoul, Tokyo — share photos if you have visited one; students respond well to personal stories).
  3. Video + notes (15 min): Mumbai video — students take 10 dot points of notes each.
  4. Main task (10 min): instructions below.
  5. Exit ticket (5 min): students write one Kahoot-style quiz question (with answer) from their video notes — collect these; they make a ready-made revision Kahoot.

Main task instructions

  1. Research task: students list the world’s current top 10 megacities (city, country, population). Any reputable source; populations vary between sources — that itself is worth a brief discussion (how do you count a city?).
  2. Plot the top 10 on a world map and describe the pattern in one sentence (most are in Asia).

External links

  • Search YouTube: “Mumbai megacity documentary” (preview first; several suitable options)
  • Our World in Data — megacities visualisation: ourworldindata.org/urbanization

Cities of 10+ million people only work because of extraordinary human ingenuity. Discuss: what has to be designed well for 10 million people to live together?

LESSON 5

Key Issues for Megacities

Learning intention: we are learning the eight key issues facing megacities and why rapid growth causes them.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): return to last week’s “school ×5” brainstorm — “which of your predicted problems do you think real megacities actually have?”
  2. Teach (20 min): the eight key issues, each defined (these definitions are in the Read First PDF): urban sprawl (spread of urban areas into surrounding rural land); lack of infrastructure (poor planning/lack of basic systems, e.g. transport and power); poor public transport; air pollution and waste (contamination; poor rubbish management); burden on natural resources; large ecological footprint (land/resources required per person); informal economy (economic activity outside formal arrangements); urban poverty (weak/hazardous living conditions — sanitation, employment, security).
  3. India focus (10 min): density 382 people/km²; 17.1% of the world’s population. Class discussion: advantages AND disadvantages of urbanisation — not all change is negative.
  4. Main task (15 min): “Reducing congestion in cities” worksheet.
  5. Exit ticket (5 min): match four issues to their definitions.

Main task instructions

  1. Complete the “Reducing congestion in cities” worksheet (in the unit resources).
  2. Then: students pick the TWO issues they think are most serious for a named megacity and justify their ranking in 2–3 sentences.

External links

  • Unit folder: 2026_05_20 Read First.pdf — the printed “Key Issues for Megacities” content this lesson is built from

LESSON 6

Asian Megacity Case Study + Ecological Footprint

Learning intention: we are learning to write a structured case study of one Asian megacity, and to measure our own ecological footprint.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): students choose their Asian megacity (offer the options from the Megacity Expanded Teaching Guide — teacher briefing notes exist for each option, so steer students towards those cities).
  2. Main task 1 (25 min): structured four-part case study response (instructions below).
  3. Main task 2 (15 min): WWF Ecological Footprint Calculator — students complete the calculator and record their result, then write a 2–3 sentence reflection: what surprised you? What one change would shrink your footprint most?
  4. Consolidation (homework or spare time): summary poster covering urbanisation, rural–urban migration, international migration and push/pull factors.

Main task instructions

  1. Students write a structured four-part response on their chosen Asian megacity, using the teacher briefing notes in the Megacity Expanded Teaching Guide to support each city option.
  2. The four parts: (1) locate and describe the city; (2) explain why it grew; (3) describe two key issues it faces (use the eight from L5); (4) describe one strategy being used to manage an issue, with a brief judgement of whether it is working.
  3. Support version: sentence starters per part. Extension: compare two megacities’ responses to the same issue.

External links

  • WWF Ecological Footprint Calculator — wwf.org.au (search “ecological footprint calculator”)
  • Unit folder: Megacity_Expanded_Teaching_Guide.docx — briefing notes for every city option
