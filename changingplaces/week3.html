Stage 5 Geography | Year 9 | Changing Places

Week 3 — Australia’s Population

Syllabus focus: urban settlement patterns; Australia’s urban future. Outcomes: GE5-2, GE5-5, GE5-7, GE5-8. Threads: Caretaking Earth, Embracing Diversity.

Cultural protocols for L8 (please read first): use respectful, strengths-based, present-tense language — connection to Country is ongoing and contemporary, not a historical relic. Capitalise Country, Aboriginal, First Nations. Do not retell specific cultural stories; point students to authoritative sources instead (AIATSIS, Common Ground). The AIATSIS map shows general locations, not exact boundaries.
Week at a glance: L7 teaches the crucial difference between population size and population density (formula Dp = N ÷ A) and asks why 95% of Australia is empty; L8 is the First Nations Map of Australia worksheet (timed for NAIDOC week); L9 looks at ABS projections and sustainable responses, ending with the Narara Eco-Village case study — which is local to the Central Coast.
LESSON 7

Where Do Australians Live and Why?

Learning intention: we are learning the difference between population size and population density, and why Australians live where they do.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): “Australia is huge and rich — why doesn’t it have a billion people?” Collect theories on the board.
  2. Teach (15 min): population vs population density. Formula on the board: Dp = N ÷ A (density = population ÷ area). Worked comparisons: Australia 27.72 million people at 3.6 people/km² vs Japan 123.1 million at 340 people/km². The Woolloomooloo example: a tiny suburb can be “denser” than a whole country. State-by-state comparison task (NT → WA).
  3. Main task 1 (10 min): Population Worksheet pp. 1–4 — density calculations for several countries.
  4. Video + writing (20 min): “Why 95% of Australia is empty” documentary + paragraph response: why is the population distributed this way (climate, water, soil, history)?
  5. Exit ticket (5 min): calculate the density of a made-up country (e.g. 5 million people, 250,000 km²).

Main task instructions

  1. Complete the density calculations on the Population Worksheet using Dp = N ÷ A — check units (people per km²).
  2. Complete the “Where Do We Live? Why?” worksheet.
  3. Paragraph response to the documentary: “Explain why 95% of Australia is empty.” Require at least two physical factors and one historical factor.

External links

  • ABS — abs.gov.au (current population clock and statistics)
  • YouTube: “Why 95% of Australia is empty” (RealLifeLore or similar; preview first)

LESSON 8

First Nations Map of Australia

Learning intention: we are learning that Australia has always been a continent of hundreds of nations, and that displacement is part of the story of Australia’s changing places.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): display the AIATSIS Map of Indigenous Australia next to a state-borders map. “What is different about how these two maps divide the continent?”
  2. Teach (10 min): the map shows the general locations of hundreds of First Nations language, social and nation groups. Model how to use it respectfully (general locations, not exact boundaries). Identify the Country your school stands on.
  3. Worldview (5 min): Micah 6:8 discussion (see below).
  4. Main task (30 min): First Nations Map of Australia worksheet — four sections (instructions below). This is timed for NAIDOC week.
  5. Exit ticket (5 min): name the Country the school stands on and one bordering nation.

Main task instructions

  1. Section 1 — Knowledge & Understanding: how many nations/language groups does the map show? What does this tell us about diversity before 1788?
  2. Section 2 — Sense of Place: identify your own local Country and its bordering nations.
  3. Section 3 — Changing Places: push, pull and movement — how did colonisation, government policy and urbanisation displace First Nations peoples, and how does displacement affect identity and belonging? (This connects directly to the unit’s push/pull language: displacement is forced movement — push without pull.)
  4. Section 4 — Critical Thinking extension: why are naming, borders and acknowledgement geographical acts?

External links

  • AIATSIS Map of Indigenous Australia — aiatsis.gov.au/explore/map-indigenous-australia
  • Common Ground — commonground.org.au

LESSON 9

A Sustainable Australia

Learning intention: we are learning how Australia’s population is projected to grow and how communities can grow sustainably.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): the growth formula on the board: population increases when Births + Immigration > Deaths + Emigration. Quick check: what happens if the reverse is true?
  2. Teach (10 min): ABS projections — 50+ million Australians by 2101, based on ~1.8 babies/woman, ~240,000 immigration/year and rising life expectancy. Students interpret the projection graphs.
  3. Brainstorm (10 min): how can Australia support a growing population sustainably? Steer towards: community-level (city farms — 70+ community gardens in Sydney alone, green public space, cycling lanes, higher-density housing) and individual (walking/cycling, solar, correct waste disposal, saving water/electricity).
  4. Main task (20 min): Narara Eco-Village case study (instructions below) — this is on the Central Coast, so it is a local case study; some students may know it.
  5. Exit ticket (5 min): one community-level and one individual sustainability action.

Main task instructions

  1. Narara Eco-Village structured questions: (1) what is sociocracy? (2) how are the houses sustainable? (3) how does the village meet its environmental, social and economic sustainability goals?
  2. Personal reflection: would you prefer to live in an eco-village or a conventional suburb? Justify with at least two reasons.
  3. Extension: independent research using Our World in Data and the ABS website.

External links

  • Narara Eco-Village — nararaecovillage.com
  • ABS population projections — abs.gov.au
  • Our World in Data — ourworldindata.org
