Week 1 — Urbanisation: The World Moves to the City

Syllabus focus: causes and consequences of urbanisation. Outcomes: GE5-2, GE5-3, GE5-8. Threads: Embracing Diversity.

Week at a glance: L1 defines urbanisation and the difference between urban and rural places; L2 introduces push and pull factors — the engine of the whole unit; L3 unpacks the three causes of urban growth and uses the “school ×5” thought experiment to preview the impacts of rapid growth.
LESSON 1

What is Urbanisation?

Learning intention: we are learning what urbanisation is and how urban and rural places differ.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): ask “what changed in Britain during industrialisation?” (links to the History topic just finished). Draw out: people moved off farms and into cities for factory work. That process — urbanisation — is what this whole unit is about.
  2. Teach (15 min): definition — urbanisation is the increase in the proportion of people living in urban areas compared to rural areas. Key statistics to put on the board: 55–56% of the world now lives in urban areas (100 years ago it was 20%); projected 68% by 2050; urbanisation is fastest in developing countries (e.g. South Africa).
  3. Worldview (5 min): Genesis 12:1 discussion (see below).
  4. Main task (20 min): definition-building task (instructions below).
  5. Exit ticket (5 min): one sentence — “urbanisation is…” in the student’s own words, plus one statistic from memory.

Main task instructions

  1. Students build their own definitions of an urban area and a rural area using five criteria: population density, environment type, social diversity, infrastructure, lifestyle.
  2. Model answers to steer towards — urban: high population density, built environment, high social diversity, lots of infrastructure, fast-paced lifestyle. Rural: low density, natural environment, lower social diversity, minimal infrastructure, relaxed lifestyle.
  3. Students record both definitions in a comparison table, then write 2–3 sentences: “Which would you rather live in, and why?”

LESSON 2

Push and Pull Factors

Learning intention: we are learning to explain why people move, using push factors (reasons to leave) and pull factors (reasons to arrive).

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): quick poll — “has your family always lived here? What brought you (or kept you) here?”
  2. Teach (10 min): definitions for students to copy: push factors encourage people to leave a location (poverty, famine, civil unrest, lack of jobs); pull factors encourage people to choose a new location (jobs, shelter, protection, better quality of life). UN estimate: 90% of world population growth is happening in the cities of developing nations.
  3. Worldview (5 min): Ruth 1 discussion (see below).
  4. Main task (25 min): push/pull table + modelled paragraph (instructions below).
  5. Exit ticket (5 min): students give one push and one pull factor for their own family’s location.

Main task instructions

  1. Students draw a two-column table (PUSH | PULL) and list as many factors as they can — aim for at least six per column.
  2. Add a row for their own family: what pushes/pulls them to live where they live?
  3. Display the modelled high-level paragraph (WAGOLL) linking economic and social opportunities to increased urbanisation. Students annotate what makes it strong (topic sentence, evidence, linking word, judgement).
  4. Students write their own paragraph: “Explain the connection between economic opportunities, social opportunities and increased urbanisation.”

LESSON 3

Three Causes of Urban Growth

Learning intention: we are learning the three processes that make cities grow: rural–urban migration, international migration, and natural increase.

Lesson outline

  1. Starter (5 min): “If a city gets bigger, where do the extra people come from?” — collect answers, sort into the three causes.
  2. Teach (15 min): the three causes, with notes for students to copy: (1) rural–urban migration — people move from country to city; the rate is a combination of push and pull factors; economic (more jobs, better pay → economic growth → even more jobs) and social (belief that cities offer a better quality of life, magnified in developing countries); (2) international migration — arrivals from other countries mostly settle in cities; (3) natural increase — birth rate up, death rate down.
  3. Worldview (5 min): Jeremiah 29:7 discussion (see below).
  4. Main task (20 min): the “school ×5” thought experiment (instructions below).
  5. Exit ticket (5 min): name the three causes without notes.

Main task instructions

  1. In small groups: “Imagine the number of students in this school increased five times overnight.” Brainstorm as many impacts, challenges or changes as possible.
  2. Categorise every impact into: social (people’s lives, connections, harmony), economic (jobs and income), environmental (plants, animals, water, air).
  3. Class discussion: how do these same challenges apply to rapid urbanisation in real cities? Keep this brainstorm — it is the springboard for megacities next week.
